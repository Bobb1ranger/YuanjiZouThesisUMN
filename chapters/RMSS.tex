\chapter{Robust Mean Square Stability}
\label{chap:RMSS}
In this chapter, we first recapitulate the classical $\mu$-analysis and the definition of MS norm and MS stability~\cite{Elia2005stabfading}. We extend the notion of $\mu$-analysis to include both deterministic modeling error and stochastic process uncertainties. Inspired by the idea of relating performance to robustness, we introduce the robust MS norm to represent the system's MS performance upper bound with the presence of norm-bounded deterministic modeling error. We establish the extended \(\mu<1\) (Robust Mean Square Stable) condition and relate it to the optimally-weighted robust MS norm of the nominal model. We provide a convex optimization method to compute the tight robust MS norm upper bound and further establish a sufficient convex method for evaluating the newly-proposed \(\mu\) in robust MS stability analysis.

We validate our analysis through a classic pendulum-cart robot with a \(\mathcal{H}_\infty\) optimal controller and observe the trade-offs between deterministic modeling error and stochastic process uncertainties.

\section{Stochastic linear system robustness analysis and synthesis literature review}

In control theory, robust control is central to both theory and practice. 
Robust control analysis provides stability guarantees for a controlled system with norm-bounded modeling error, including unmodeled dynamics and real parametric uncertainty, which are unknown yet deterministic~\cite{Zhou1996RobustControl, Doyle1987UncertainSS, Packard1990QuadStab, Gahinet1994LMIHinf,packard_mainloop}.

Networked systems have drawn attention to stochastic systems and processes~\cite{IntroStoc}. In particular, stochastic stability problems arise from real-time uncertainties in the process, such as communication intermittency between subsystems and multiplicative noise. However, existing robustness analysis approaches can't effectively handle those real-time uncertainties arising from stochastic processes. To enlarge the scope of robust stability for stochastic processes, the notion of Mean Square (MS) stability is more relevant and has therefore been studied more extensively~\cite{IntroStoc, Willems1973MSstability, Willems1971FreqMSS, Elia2005stabfading}.
\cite{Willems1971FreqMSS, Willems1973MSstability} found state-space and frequency domain necessary and sufficient conditions for MS stability with a single multiplicative white-noise channel.


\cite{Elia2005stabfading} has first proposed a robust control framework for the remote stabilization of MIMO LTI systems with signal transmissions are subject to various stochastic dropout or fading. It derives a necessary and sufficient condition for MS stability, showing that MS stability can be equivalently thought of as robust mean stability. The framework has been extended to various network control settings \cite{Padma2006tradeoffs, Padma06ACC, Jeff11, MattACC15, Matt2017thesis} which are relevant in this paper. In particular, \cite{Ma2015} is the first to show that MS performance is equivalent to an augmented MSS problem in the same vain of the analogous robust performance problems for deterministic uncertainty. 

Meanwhile, A control system need to tolerate and even actively compensate intermittent communications beyond those accounted for in the nominal control design. A common practice is to monitor the status of communication channels and impute missing signals accordingly. \cite{Matt2014cvxMScontrol, Matt2014cvxMScontrol_act, Matt2017thesis} demonstrate that this compensation mechanism can be formulated as a linear component and synthesized via convex optimization for a large class of systems. 

Despite these advances, existing mean square stability results typically assume perfect knowledge of the nominal system model, thereby neglecting classical modeling uncertainties. The interaction between deterministic modeling errors and stochastic process uncertainties remains insufficiently understood. In this chapter, we aim to investigate this interplay and develop a unified robustness framework that simultaneously accounts for modeling errors and process stochasticity.

Several approaches have sought to capture the interplay between deterministic and stochastic uncertainties from different directions. \cite{Seiler2003BRLjs} investigated the mean $\mathcal{H}_\infty$ performance of a Markovian jumping system. The expected \(\mathcal{H}_\infty\) norm provides a sufficient robustness margin of the jumping system. \cite{Seiler2005HinfNC, Stab_MJLS_1} and \cite{Stab_MJLS_2} established robust stabilizing control synthesis for such jumping systems under various assumptions. \cite{MixH_2infcontrolforrandomsys} considered random time delay network systems and optimized their mixed $\mathcal{H}_2$/$\mathcal{H}_\infty$ performance. \cite{CtrlPackDrop_uncertain} considered the norm-bounded model uncertainty in the random packet drop problem and found a sufficient condition for different controller schemes to ensure stability.
Recently, some researchers have observed similarities between robust stability and stochastic stability and have designed MS stabilizing controllers for stochastic systems using robust control methods~\cite{RobustControl_Mulnoise}.  





\section{Problem Formulation}
In this research, we consider the dynamical system with both model uncertainty and stochastic multiplicative channels. It is characterized as a nominal system \(M\) interconnecting with a \(\Delta \in \bm{\Delta}\) representing model uncertainty and \(\bar{\Delta}\) representing the stochastic gain. We'd like to establish the robust stability analysis of such dynamical systems and extend the classical \(\mu\)-analysis.
\begin{problem}

    \label{fig:sysconstr}
\end{figure}
Determine if the uncertain stochastic dynamical system \(\mathcal{F}_l(\mathcal{F}_u(M,\Delta),\bar{\Delta})\), with block diagram shown in Figure~\ref{fig:sysconstr},
\begin{subequations}\label{Discrete_statespace_Mix}
\begin{align}
M\colon x[k+1]& ={A}x[k]+{B_p}p[k]+{B_v}v[k]\\
q[k] &={C_q}x[k] +{D_{qp}}p[k]+{D_{qv}}v[k]\\
y[k] &={C_y}x[k]+{D_{yp}}p[k]+{D_{yv}}v[k]
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
interconnecting with $\forall\; \Delta(\colon q \mapsto p) \in \mathcal{B}_{\bm{\Delta}}^{\mathrm{LTI}}$ and \(\forall\; \bar{\Delta}(\colon y \mapsto v) \in \mathcal{B}_{\bar{\Delta}}^{\mathrm{sto}}\) is mean square stable. 
Mathematically, determine if the expected second moment matrix of the states \(\E\{x[k]x[k]^\top\}\to \mathbf{0}\) as \(k \to \infty\).
\end{problem}

\begin{rem}
Without loss of generality, the sizes of all uncertainty blocks can be normalized to be bounded by one. Indeed, any scaling in the magnitude of the uncertainties can be absorbed into the nominal system through an appropriate rescaling. Therefore, it is sufficient to consider uncertainty sets satisfying \(\|\Delta\|\le 1\).

In this formulation, \(\mathcal{B}_{\bm{\Delta}}^{\mathrm{LTI}}\) represent a class of linear-time-invariant uncertainty with \(\mathcal{H}_\infty\) norm bounded by \(1\), elaborated in subsection~\ref{subsec:mu}. 
\(\mathcal{B}_{\bar{\Delta}}^{\mathrm{sto}}\) is a class of multi-channel random gain with zeros mean and variance not greater than \(1\), elaborated in subsection~\ref{subsec:MSS}.
\end{rem}

The closed loop system in Figure~\ref{fig:interconnection} is well-posed and internally stable for all $\Delta\in\mathcal{B}_\Delta^{\mathrm{LTI}}$ iff $\mu_{\bm{\Delta}}(M)<1$. 
\end{proposition}


\begin{defn}
A class of structure $\bm{\Delta}_{\mathrm{LTI}}$ is said to be $\mu$-simple if $2L+F\leq 2$.
\end{defn}
The following result provides a method to compute \(\mu_{\bm{\Delta}}(M)\) given an arbitrary $\mu$-simple \(\bm{\Delta}\) structure.
\begin{thm}\label{thm:musimple}
If $\bm{\Delta}_{\mathrm{LTI}}$ is $\mu$-simple, \(\mu_{\bm{\Delta}}(M)\) can be found by solving the following optimization:
\[
\mu_{\bm{\Delta}}(M) = \inf_{D\in \mathcal{D}}\bar{\sigma}(D^{\frac{1}{2}}MD^{-\frac{1}{2}}),
\]
where \(\mathcal{D}\) is the set of square matrices that commute with any generic element in \(\bm{\Delta}\), 
\begin{equation*}
    \mathcal{D} \coloneqq \{\mathrm{diag}(D_1,\hdots,D_L,d_1 I_{m_1},\hdots,d_F I_{m_F}),\;
    D_i \in \mathbb{S}^{r_i}, d_j>0\}.
\end{equation*}
Otherwise, the above optimization problem finds an upper bound for \(\mu_{\bm{\Delta}}(M)\).   
\end{thm}

\subsection{Mean Square(MS) stability and MS norm} \label{subsec:MSS}

In this section, we briefly review some results on mean-square stability, first presented in \cite{Elia2005stabfading}. For the related continuous-time result, see \cite{Vaidya2018CtMSS}.
In particular, IID random variables can be interpreted as stochastic uncertainty in the familiar robust control framework. We consider systems in discrete-time.

\begin{figure}[h]\label{fig:sysconstr_elia}
    \centering
        \includegraphics[scale = 0.6]{figures/RMSSfigure/MSS_intercon.png}
        \caption[LTI plant interconnected with stochastic gain channels]{The system is constructed with a nominal plant and a random gain feedback between inputs and outputs. $\bar{\Delta}$ denotes the random gain block, defined in following}
\end{figure}

\cite{Elia2005stabfading} considered an LTI system with a stochastic perturbation interconnecting  output $y$ and input $v$. $v=\bar{\Delta}y$, with the stochastic $\bar{\Delta}$ satisfies the following assumptions:
\begin{ass}\label{asp:MSS_stocDelta}
$\bar{\Delta}$ is a diagonal multiplicative operator. Mathematically, $\bar{\Delta}=\mathrm{diag}(\delta_{s_i},i=1,...,n_v)$. $v=\bar{\Delta}y \Leftrightarrow v_i=\delta_{s_i} y_i$ for each \(i=1,\hdots,n_v\).
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\delta_{s_i}[k]$ and $\delta_{s_j}[k]$ are independent for $i\neq j$.
    \item $\delta_{s_i}[k]$ are I.I.D. in time, and independent of the system process \(x[0\colon t]\) for any \(t\in \mathbb{N}\).
    \item $\E\{\delta_{s_i}[k]\}=0$ in each channel.
    \item $var(\delta_{s_i}[k])=\sigma^2$ in each channel.    
\end{enumerate}
We denote the set of stochastic uncertainty above with $\sigma^2\leq 1$ as 
${\cal B}_{\bar{\Delta}}^{\mathrm{sto}}$.
\end{ass}



\begin{defn}
Given an LTI system \(G\) with state-space realization:
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:DT_statespace}
G: {x^+}={A}x+{B}{v};\quad {y}=C x+D v; x[0] = x_0.
\end{equation}
Its feedback interconnection with $v =\bar{\Delta} y$ is said to be MS stable if the states' expected second moment matrix $M[k] \coloneqq \E\{x[k]x[k]^T\}$ is well defined for all \(k \in \mathbb{N}\), and $\lim_{k\to\infty}M[k]=\mathbf{0}$.
\end{defn}
\begin{rem}
The states' moment matrix \(M[k]\) will asymptotically converge to zero because there is no additive noise in the dynamics by the system in~\eqref{eqn:DT_statespace}. The Mean Square Stability implies that \(G\) is Schur stable, i.e. $\rho(A)<1$.
\end{rem}





Then open loop transfer matrix mapping $v$ to $y$ is therefore
$G(z)=C(zI-A)^{-1}B+D$, a $n_y\times n_v$ (We require that $n_y = n_v$) transfer function matrix. We now introduce the mean square norm, an open-loop performance index that is closely related to the analysis of a system’s closed-loop mean square stability:
\begin{defn}\label{defn:MSnorm}
The MS norm of $p\times p$ transfer function matrix $G=[G_{ij}]$ ($G_{ij}$ are proper transfer functions.) is defined as 
$$
\|G\|_{MS} \doteq \max_{i=1,\hdots,p}\sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^p{\|G_{ij}\|_2^2}}  
$$
\end{defn}
Note that for a given explicit transfer matrix, MS norm gives the maximum among the output channels while the traditional $\mathcal{H}_2$ norm gives the sum energies of the output channels.

Motivated by the definition of MS stability and classical \(\mu\)-analysis, \cite{Elia2005stabfading} further defines the MS structured norm by the largest perturbation `size' that doesn't destabilize the system, measured in terms of the variance of \(\bar{\Delta}\). 
\begin{defn}\label{defn:muMS}
The Mean Square structured norm of a system $G$ with stochastic interconnection $\bar{\Delta}$, denoted by $\mu_{\mathrm{MS}}(G,\bar{\Delta})$ is defined as
\begin{equation*}
    \mu_{\mathrm{MS}}(G,\bar{\Delta})=\frac{1}{\sup\{\sigma^2:\text{the closed loop system is MS stable}\}}
\end{equation*}
\end{defn}
This definition also implies that $\frac{1}{\mu_{\mathrm{MS}}}$ is the maximal variance the system can tolerate in the stochastic feedback channel. \(\mu_{\mathrm{MS}} < 1\) ensures that the interconnection of \(G\) with any \(\bar{\Delta} \in {\cal B}_{\bar{\Delta}}^{\mathrm{sto}}\) is MS stable.

For simplicity, Assumption~\ref{asp:MSS_plant} is made to ensure the plant is stable by itself and its interconnection with \(\bar{\Delta}\) is always well-posed. \cite{Elia2005stabfading} further provides the following core theorem on $\mu_{MS}$.
\begin{ass}\label{asp:MSS_plant}
\(D\) is either strictly upper or lower triangular.
\end{ass}

\begin{thm}\label{thm:MSS}
Under Assumption~\ref{asp:MSS_plant} and \ref{asp:MSS_stocDelta}, the following statements are equivalent:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The feedback interconnection of $G$ and $\bar{\Delta}$ is MS stable.
    \item There exist a positive definite matrix $Q$ and positive vector $\alpha\in \mathbb{R}^p$such that 
    \begin{subequations}
    \begin{align}
        Q & > A Q A^T+\sum_{j=1}^p B_j \alpha_j B_j^T\\
        \alpha_i&> \sigma^2(C_i Q C_i^T+\sum_{j=1}^p D_{ij}\alpha_j D_{ij}^T),\; \forall\ i=1,\hdots,p
    \end{align}
    \end{subequations}
    % \item $\sigma^2 \inf_{\theta>0,\mathrm{diag}}\|\theta G \theta^{-1}\|_{MS}^2<1$
    \item $\sigma^2\rho(\tilde{G})<1$, where \(\tilde{G}\) is the element-wise \(\mathcal{H}_2\) norm matrix of LTI system \(G\):
    \begin{equation}   
    \tilde{G} \doteq
    \left[
    \begin{array}{ccc}
        \|G_{11}\|_2^2 & \ldots & \|G_{1 n_v}\|_2^2\\
        \vdots & \ddots &\vdots\\
        \|G_{{n_y}1}\|_2^2 & \ldots & \|G_{{n_y}{n_v}}\|_2^2
    \end{array}
    \right].
    \end{equation}
Note that the notation $\tilde{\cdot}$ is used throughout this chapter to denote the element-wise $\mathcal{H}_2$ norm matrix of an LTI system.
\end{enumerate}
\end{thm}
Recalling that the spectral radius \(\rho(\cdot)\) of a matrix  is defined as its largest eigenvalue. Since \(\tilde{G}\) is a non-negative valued matrix, 
Corollary~\ref{cor:muMScompute} follows immediately,
\begin{cor}\label{cor:muMScompute}
\begin{equation}
\mu_{\rm{MS}}=\rho(\tilde{G})=\inf_{\theta>0,\mathrm{diag}}\|\theta G \theta^{-1}\|_{\rm{MS}}^2
    =\inf_{\theta>0,\mathrm{diag}} \max_{i}\sum_{j}\|\theta_{i}G_{ij}\theta_j^{-1}\|_2^2
\end{equation}
\end{cor}


% And the MS stability is defined as: $\lim_{k \rightarrow \infty}\E(x(k)x(k)^T)$ for following system.


The above results show that the condition for MS stability in the presence of I.I.D. stochastic uncertainty has several similarities with other robust stability results. The spectral radius condition, in terms of the $\ell_1$ norms instead of the ${\cal H}_2$ norm squared, is necessary and sufficient for the robust stability w.r.t. model uncertainty of bounded  $\ell_\infty$ induced norm~\cite{Khammash1990Robustl1}. The minimization over constant $\theta$ weight is analogous to Theorem~\ref{thm:musimple}, with the ${\cal H}_\infty$ norm instead of the MS norm, which appears in the robust stability condition when the model uncertainty is nonlinear or time varying with bounded $\ell_2$ induced norm~\cite{Shamma1994RS}.

    {C_y}(z{I}-{A})^{-1}{B_p}+{D_{yp}}&{C_y}(z{I}-{A})^{-1}{B_v}+D_{yv}\\
\end{array}\right]\\
\end{equation}
We next construct a bunch of transfer functions \(M_i(z)\) from $M(z)$.
Let 
\begin{equation}\label{constrM_i}
M_i(z)=
\left[
    \begin{array}{cc}
        M_{qp}&M_{qv}\\
        M_{y_i p}&M_{y_i v}\\
    \end{array}\right]
\end{equation}
The following result is about robust ${\cal H}_2$ norm specifically for single-output systems.
\begin{lemma}\label{MISOH_2}\cite{Sznaier1999RobustH2}
Given a MISO system with input $v\in \mathbb{R}^{n_v}$ and scalar output \(y\in\mathbb{R}\). If there exist a positive definite Hermitian matrix $X(\omega)\in \mathbb{X}$ and a real transfer function $y(\omega)>0$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{freqbound}
    [M(e^{j\omega})]^\hermconj \left[
\begin{array}{cc}
    X(\omega) &  \\
     & 1
\end{array}\right]M(e^{j\omega})<\left[
\begin{array}{cc}
    X(\omega) &  \\
     & y(\omega)I_{n_v}
\end{array}\right]
\end{equation} holds for all $\omega\in[0,2\pi)$ and,
\begin{equation}\label{intbyfreq}
    \int_0^{2\pi}y(\omega)\frac{d\omega}{2\pi}<\gamma^2    
\end{equation}
Then the robust $\mathcal{H}_2$ norm of the uncertain system is bounded by $\gamma$.

\end{lemma}
The condition is necessary and sufficient when the structure of the deterministic uncertainty $\Delta$ is $\mu$-simple, i.e., $2L+F\leq2$. While extending the result to robust ${\cal H}_2$-norm of MIMO systems can be conservative (see~\cite{Sznaier2000WNrobustH2} Theorem 3), it turns out that the extension to MIMO robust MS-norm is direct. This is our main result of this subsection. 
\begin{thm}\label{thm:robMSnorm}
Nominal System $M$ has Robust MS Norm performance $<\gamma$ if there exist 
positive definite Hermitian matrices $X_i(\omega)\in \mathbb{X}$ and real transfer function $y_i(\omega)>0$
$i=1,\hdots,n_v$ such that 
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:freqbound_2}
    [M_i(e^{j\omega})]^\hermconj\left[
\begin{array}{cc}
    X_i(\omega) &  \\
     & 1
\end{array}\right]M_i(e^{j\omega})<\left[
\begin{array}{cc}
    X_i(\omega) &  \\
     & y_i(\omega)I_{n_v}
\end{array}\right]
\end{equation} holds for all $\omega\in[0,2\pi)$ and,
\begin{equation}\label{eqn:intbyfreq_2}
    \int_0^{2\pi}y_i(\omega)\frac{d\omega}{2\pi}<\gamma^2.
\end{equation}
The condition is necessary and sufficient when the structure of the deterministic uncertainty $\Delta$ is $\mu$ simple, which is satisfying $2L+F\leq2$.
\end{thm}


\begin{proof}
Proof of Sufficiency:
Assume there are $X_i(\omega),y_i(\omega),\; \forall i\in\{1,\hdots,n_v\}$ satisfying the collection of LMIs in~\eqref{eqn:freqbound_2} and \eqref{eqn:intbyfreq_2}.
Then, for each MISO sub-system with input $v$ and output $y_i$, apply Lemma~\ref{MISOH_2}; each of the systems has robust $H_2$ performance less than $\gamma$. According to the definition of MS norm of a MIMO system to be the largest $H_2$ norm of all its output channels, the robust MS norm performance of system $M$ is less than $\gamma$.

Proof of Necessity (Assuming the structure of the deterministic uncertainty to be $\mu$-simple):
From the definition of MS norm, each output channel has a robust $\mathcal{H}_2$ norm less than $\gamma$ when the robust MS norm of system $M$ is less than $\gamma$.
% (Otherwise, we can pick the $\Delta$ which makes one of the output channel have $H_2$ norm more than $\gamma$. Then the robust MS norm of $M$ is more than $\gamma$).

Because Lemma~\ref{MISOH_2} is sufficient and necessary when $\Delta$ is $\mu$-simple. We can always find a pair of $X$ and $y$ that satisfy the LMIs \eqref{eqn:freqbound_2} and \eqref{eqn:intbyfreq_2} if one MISO system has robust $H_2$ performance less than $\gamma$.
Therefore, for each output channel, there exists such a pair of functions of frequency $X_i(\omega)$ and $y_i(\omega)$. 
\end{proof}
% The proof is based on applying Lemma \ref{MISOH_2} on each output $y_i$.  
% In principle, we should only allow one same perturbation $\Delta$ for each of the $M_i$'s. However, given that our performance measure is the max 2-norm  of each $M_i$ and that $\Delta$ sees the same transfer function matrix for each $i$, there is no conservatism induced by searching over worst case $\Delta_i$ for each subsystem $i$. 




\subsection{Robust MS stability}


Mimicking the structured singular value for multi-block uncertainty, we define the $\mu$ value of a nominal system $M$ with respect to a class of LTI uncertainties interconnected with $\diag(\Delta,\bar{\Delta})$. When both types of uncertainties are present, it is generally difficult to obtain analytical results comparable to Theorems~\ref{thm:musimple} and~\ref{thm:MSS}. 

To address this challenge, we adopt a sequential loop-closing strategy. In \cite{Seiler2003BRLjs}, the loop is first closed with the stochastic uncertainty, and it is shown that the inverse of the expected $\mathcal{H}_\infty$ norm provides a sufficient lower bound on the size of uncertainties that preserve robust stability. In contrast, we first close the loop with $\Delta$ and then evaluate the resulting system in terms of its robust mean square performance. This approach leads to a necessary and sufficient condition for robust mean square stability.

\begin{defn}
Let $G_\Delta=\mathcal{F}_u(M,\Delta)$ be the upper LFT of $M$ and $\Delta$, Define 
$$
\mu_{\bm{\Delta},\bar{\Delta}} (M)= 
\begin{cases}
    \mu_{\bm{\Delta}}(M_{qp})& \text{ if }\mu_{\bm{\Delta}}(M_{qp})\geq 1\\
    \max\{\mu_{\bm{\Delta}}(M_{qp}),\;\max_{\Delta\in\mathcal{B}_\Delta^{\mathrm{LTI}}}\mu_{\mathrm{MS}}(G_{\Delta})\}& \text{else}
\end{cases}.
$$

\end{defn}

\begin{thm}
$\mu_{\bm{\Delta},\bar{\Delta}} (M)<1$ if and only if the $\mathcal{F}_u(M,\Delta)$ is well defined and robustly stable and $\mathcal{F}_l(\mathcal{F}_u(M,\Delta),\bar{\Delta})$ is MS stable for all $\Delta\in\mathcal{B}_\Delta^{\mathrm{LTI}}$ and all $\bar{\Delta}$ with $\sigma^2\leq1$.
\end{thm}
\begin{proof}
Proof of sufficiency: 
By the definition of \(\mu_{\bm{\Delta},\bar{\Delta}}(M)<1\), 
\[
\mu_{\bm{\Delta}}(M_{qp})<1,\quad  \max_{\Delta\in\mathcal{B}_\Delta^{\mathrm{LTI}}}\mu_{\rm{MS}}(G_{\Delta})<1.
\] 
Therefore, $G_{\Delta} = \mathcal{F}_u(M, \Delta)$ is well defined and robustly stable for all $\Delta\in\mathcal{B}_\Delta^{\mathrm{LTI}}$. For all $\Delta\in\mathcal{B}_\Delta^{\mathrm{LTI}}$, the system $\mu_{\rm{MS}}(G_\Delta)<1$, which means $G_{\Delta}$ is MS stable for all $\bar{\Delta}$ with variance $\sigma^2\leq1$.

Proof of necessity: If $\mu_{\bm{\Delta},\bar{\Delta}} (M)\geq1$, then $\mu_{\bm{\Delta}}(M_{qp})\geq1$ or $\mu_{\bm{\Delta}}(M_{qp})<1$ and $\max_{\Delta\in\mathcal{B}_\Delta^{\mathrm{LTI}}}\mu_{\rm{MS}}(G_{\Delta})\ge 1$. If $\mu_{\bm{\Delta}}(M_{qp})\geq1$, then $\mathcal{F}_u(M, \Delta)$ is not well defined or unstable for some $\Delta\in\mathcal{B}_\Delta^{\mathrm{LTI}}$. Otherwise, $\max_{\Delta\in\mathcal{B}_\Delta^{\mathrm{LTI}}}\mu_{\rm{MS}}(G_{\Delta})\geq1$. Note $G_{\Delta}$ is well defined and stable for all $\Delta\in\mathcal{B}_\Delta^{\mathrm{LTI}}$ in this case. Then for some ${\Delta}\in\mathcal{B}_\Delta^{\mathrm{LTI}}$, there exist $\bar{\Delta}$ with variance $\sigma^2\leq1$, which makes $G_\Delta$ MS unstable.
\end{proof}

Given the significance of MS-norm in the MS stability condition of Corollary~\ref{cor:muMScompute}, in this section, we consider the problem of robust MS stability. In analogy with the main loop theorem in \cite{packard_mainloop}, we have the following corollary,
\begin{cor}\label{cor:MS_specr}
Given a nominal system $M$ defined as \ref{Discrete_statespace_Mix}. Assume $G_\Delta= \mathcal{F}_u(M, \Delta)$ is well defined and robustly stable for $\Delta\in\mathcal{B}_\Delta^{\mathrm{LTI}}$. Then \(\mu_{\bm{\Delta},\bar{\Delta}} (M) < 1\) if and only if 
\begin{equation*}
    \rho_{r}(\tilde{G})\doteq \max_{\Delta \in \mathcal{B}_{\bm{\Delta}}^{\mathrm{LTI}}}\rho(\tilde{G}_{\Delta}) < 1.
\end{equation*}
Here, the subscript $r$ denotes robustness.
\end{cor}
% Note is $M$ has robust spectral radius $\rho(\tilde{G})_r\geq 1 $ iff the feedback interconnection of $M$ with 
% $\Delta_{mix}=\left[\begin{array}{cc}\Delta&\\& \bar{\Delta}\end{array}\right]$ is  MS stable for all $\Delta\in \mathcal{B}_{\bm{\Delta}}^{\mathrm{LTI}}$ and $\bar{\Delta}\in {\cal B}_{\bar{\Delta}}^{stoc}$. 

Applying Corollary~\ref{cor:muMScompute}, for a fixed $\Delta$, the quantity 
$\rho(\tilde{G}_\Delta)$ can be computed by solving
\[
\rho(\tilde{G}_\Delta)
= \inf_{\substack{\theta \succ 0 \\ \theta \in \mathrm{Diag}}}
\left\| \theta G_\Delta \theta^{-1} \right\|_{\mathrm{MS}}^2.
\]
The quantity $\rho_r(\tilde{G})$ is defined as the maximum of these optimal values 
over all $\Delta\in\mathcal{B}_{\bm{\Delta}}^{\mathrm{LTI}}$. Computing the exact value of $\rho_r(\tilde{G})$ is 
challenging, as the $\theta$ depends on $\Delta$.

\subsection{Computational Methods}
To obtain a tractable upper bound on $\rho_r(\tilde{G})$, we introduce some conservatism by interchanging the infimum over $\theta$ with the maximum over $\Delta$. This removes the dependence of $\theta_\Delta$ on $\Delta$. We leave to future research this investigation into reducing conservatism. 
Define
$$
\bar{\rho}_r(\tilde{G}) \doteq \inf_{\theta>0}\max_{\Delta\in\mathcal{B}_\Delta^{\mathrm{LTI}}}\|\theta G_\Delta\theta^{-1}\|^2_{\rm{MS}}.
$$
The quantity $\bar{\rho}_r(\tilde{G})$ represents the lower bound of robust mean-square norm of $G_\Delta$ under a fixed scaling $\theta$.
Note that  
\begin{equation*}
\rho_r(\tilde{G})=\max_{\Delta\in\mathcal{B}_\Delta^{\mathrm{LTI}}}\inf_{\theta>0}\|\theta G_\Delta\theta^{-1}\|^2_{\rm{MS}}\leq \inf_{\theta>0}\max_{\Delta\in\mathcal{B}_\Delta^{\mathrm{LTI}}}\|\theta G_\Delta\theta^{-1}\|^2_{\rm{MS}} = \bar{\rho}_r(\tilde{G}).
\end{equation*}
\begin{rem}\label{rem:1chn}
Notably, the conservatism introduced by interchanging the infimum and supremum disappears in the single-channel stochastic uncertainty case, since the robust performance is invariant under scaling by \(\theta\).
Mathematically, if $n_v = 1$, $\rho_r(\tilde{G}) = \bar{\rho}_r(\tilde{G})$.
\end{rem}


Theorem~\ref{thm:robMSnorm} provides a computational procedure to evaluate 
$\max_{\Delta \in \mathcal{B}_\Delta^{\mathrm{LTI}}} \|\theta G_\Delta \theta^{-1}\|^2_{\rm MS}$ 
for a fixed $\theta$. Building on this result, we now present the main theorem of this section, which formulates a convex feasibility problem for verifying robust mean square stability.

\begin{thm}\label{thm:RMSSsuff}
System $M$ has $\mu_{\bm{\Delta},\bar{\Delta}}(M)<1$ if there exist 
positive definite Hermitian matrices $X_i(\omega)\in {\bf{\Lambda}}$, real-valued function $w_i(\omega)>0$ for $i=1,\hdots,n_v$ and real positive diagonal scaling $\beta$  such that 
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
M_i(\omega)
\left[
\begin{array}{cc}
    \tilde{X}_i(\omega) & 0 \\
    0 &\beta
\end{array}\right]
[M_i(\omega)]^\hermconj & <
\begin{bmatrix}
    \tilde{X}_i(\omega) & 0 \\
    0 & w_i(\omega)
\end{bmatrix}\label{eqn:mu_freq_lmi1} \\
\int_0^{2\pi}w_i(\omega)\frac{d\omega}{2\pi} & < \beta_i,\quad \forall i=1,\hdots, n_v.
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
\end{thm}
% 



\begin{proof}
First of all, LMI (\ref{eqn:mu_freq_lmi1}) implies, $M_{qp}(\omega)\tilde{X}_i(\omega)M_{qp}^*(\omega)<\tilde{X}_i(\omega)$, which ensures $\mu_{\bm{\Delta}}(M_{qp}) < 1$ for all \(\omega\in\left[0,2\pi\right)\).
To include the diagonal weights \(\theta\), we let \({y^{\star}}={\theta}{y},\ {v^{\star}}={\theta}{v}\). Let \(M^\theta\) denote the re-weighted transfer function matrix by \(\theta\):
\begin{equation}
\begin{bmatrix}
    q \\
    y^\star
\end{bmatrix}
= M^\theta 
\begin{bmatrix}
    p\\
    v^\star
\end{bmatrix}
;\;
M^\theta = \left[
    \begin{array}{cc}
        M_{qp}&M_{qv}\theta^{-1}\\
        \theta M_{yp} &\theta M_{yv}\theta^{-1}\\
    \end{array}\right].
\end{equation}
We apply Theorem \ref{thm:robMSnorm} on the re-weighted mapping. For the \(i\)-th output channel,
\begin{equation}\label{eachoutputM}
M_i^\theta = \left[
    \begin{array}{cc}
        M_{qp} & M_{qv}\theta^{-1}\\
        \theta_i M_{yp_i} &\theta_i M_{yv_i}\theta^{-1}\\
    \end{array}\right]
    = 
    \begin{bmatrix}
        I & \\
        & \theta_i
    \end{bmatrix}
    M_i 
    \begin{bmatrix}
        I & \\
        & \theta^{-1}
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
Substitute (\ref{eachoutputM}) in (\ref{freqbound}) will give us
\begin{equation}\label{freqbound_scale}
    [M_i(\omega)]^\hermconj
    \left[
    \begin{array}{cc}
    X_i(\omega) &  \\
     & \theta_i^2
    \end{array}\right]
    M_i(\omega)
<\left[
\begin{array}{cc}
    X_i(\omega) &  \\
     & y_i(\omega)\theta^2
\end{array}\right]
\end{equation}
% Note here that, for each output channel, we select a new multiplier $X_i(\omega)$, because we want to find the $\mathcal{H}_2$ norm bound, i.e. the worst case, for each channel. There's no need to restrict that the $\Delta$ hold the same.

LMI~\ref{intbyfreq} gives us that for a fixed $\theta$, the robust MS norm of the scaled system is
$$
\|\theta G \theta^{-1}\|_{MS,r}^2=\max_i{ \int_0^{2\pi}y_i(\omega)\frac{d\omega}{2\pi}}
$$
Optimized over $\theta$, $\bar{\rho}(\tilde{G})_r$ is given by
\begin{equation}
    \bar{\rho}_r(\tilde{G})=\inf_{\theta}\|\theta G \theta^{-1}\|_{MS,r}^2=\inf_{\theta}\max_i{ \int_0^{2\pi}y_i(\omega)\frac{d\omega}{2\pi}}
\end{equation}
Therefore, $\bar{\rho}_r(\tilde{G})<1$ if
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
    [M_i(\omega)]^\hermconj
    \left[
    \begin{array}{cc}
    X_i(\omega) &  \\
     & \theta_i^2
    \end{array}\right]
    M_i(\omega)
& <\left[
\begin{array}{cc}
    X_i(\omega) &  \\
     & y_i(\omega)\theta^2   
\end{array}\right] ; \\
\int_0^{2\pi}y_i(\omega)\frac{d\omega}{2\pi} & < 1,\; \forall i=1,\ldots,n_v.\label{eqn:freqsum}
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
This is equivalent to the feasibility SDP formulation:
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
    [M_i(\omega)]^\hermconj
    \left[
    \begin{array}{cc}
    X_i(\omega) &  \\
     & \theta_i^2
    \end{array}\right]
    M_i(\omega)
& <\left[
\begin{array}{cc}
    X_i(\omega) &  \\
     & \tilde{Y}_i(\omega)   
\end{array}\right] ; \\
\int_0^{2\pi} \tilde{Y}_i(\omega)\frac{d\omega}{2\pi} & < \theta^2,\; \forall i=1,\ldots,n_v.
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
To reduce the number of variables and constraints in the above feasibility SDP formulation.
By some LMI transformations, we obtain an equivalent problem in a quasi-convex form. By Schur complement, the LMI~\eqref{freqbound_scale} is equivalent to,
\begin{equation*}
    M_i
    \left[
    \begin{array}{cc}
        X_i(\omega)^{-1} & 0 \\
        0 & y_i(\omega)^{-1} \theta^{-2}
    \end{array}\right]
    M_i^*<
    \left[
    \begin{array}{cc}
        X_i(\omega)^{-1} & 0 \\
        0 & \theta_i^{-2}
    \end{array}\right] 
\end{equation*}
Multiply the scalar $y_i(\omega)$ on both sides and let $\tilde{X}_i(\omega)= X_i(\omega)^{-1}y_i(\omega)$. Since $y_i(\omega)>0$ is a scalar, \(\tilde{X}_i(\omega)\in {\bf{\Lambda}}\).
\begin{equation}
    M_i(\omega)
    \left[
    \begin{array}{cc}
        \tilde{X}_i(\omega) & 0 \\
        0 & \theta^{-2}
    \end{array}\right]
    M_i^*(\omega)<
    \left[
    \begin{array}{cc}
        \tilde{X}_i(\omega) & 0 \\
        0 & y_i(\omega)\theta_i^{-2}
    \end{array}\right]
\end{equation}
Let $\theta_i^{-2}=\beta_i$, $\beta=\mathrm{diag}(\beta_i)$ and $y_i(\omega)\theta_i^{-2}=w_i(\omega)$. \eqref{eqn:freqsum} is transformed into 
\begin{equation}
   \,\beta_i\,\geq \int_0^{2\pi}w_i(\omega)\frac{d\omega}{2\pi}
\end{equation}
The proof is complete.
\end{proof}
In Theorem~\ref{thm:RMSSsuff}, $\tilde{X}_i(\omega)$ is a frequency-dependent multiplier that enforces pointwise singular-value boundedness, whereas $\beta$ is a constant scaling factor that weights all frequencies equally, thereby enabling a correct evaluation of the mean square norm.

\section{Summary}
In this chapter, we model intermittent communication channels in dynamical systems as stochastic channels composed of a nominal mean channel in parallel with a mean-zero random gain channel. 
We introduce the concept of robust mean-square (MS) stability, which characterizes the asymptotic convergence of the state second moment in the presence of both intermittent communication and model uncertainty.  

We further extend classical $\mu$-analysis to incorporate these stochastic uncertainties and derive an efficient computational sufficient condition for guaranteeing robust MS stability. 
The proposed framework is validated on an inverted pendulum example, where measurement signals are subject to packet dropouts. 
The results quantify how different sources of uncertainty jointly degrade the system’s tolerance to each other.
